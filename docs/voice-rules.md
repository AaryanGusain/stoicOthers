# Voice rules

Everything user-facing on the four sites and in the newsletters follows the same voice. These are non-negotiable for any new copy.

---

## Hard rules

- **No em dashes (—) anywhere in your own prose.** They read as AI-written. Use commas, periods, or line breaks instead. Em dashes are allowed only inside *quoted historic text*, where they're the translator's punctuation, not yours.
- **No semicolons in your own prose.** Same exception: keep them inside quoted historic text where they appear in the translator's version.
- **No prices on the main link-in-bio page.** Prices belong on the product pages (book, audiobook, wallpapers), never on `apps/main/`.
- **No scene-setting openings.** Do not begin a piece with "It is 7:14 a.m.," "A coworker sends a one-word reply," or "Imagine you are standing at the edge of…". This pattern is the most recognizable AI tell and reads as a formula across the page.

## AI tells to avoid

These phrases are banned because they show up disproportionately in machine-generated prose:

- "In a world where…"
- "It's important to remember…"
- "Fundamentally,"
- "Navigate" as a verb for life or feelings
- "Tapestry" (of anything)
- "Delve" (into anything)
- "Journey of self-discovery" (or any "journey" used metaphorically)
- "Embark on"
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "We live in a time when…"
- Rhetorical questions to the reader ("Have you ever wondered…")
- "Remember that…" as a closer
- "The next time you…" as a closer

## Sentence shape

- Plain, declarative sentences. Short paragraphs.
- Treat the reader as an intelligent adult. No hectoring, no "you should," no implied "you'd be smarter if you read this."
- Hedges are weak: avoid "perhaps," "maybe," "in some sense," "arguably," "it could be said." Pick a claim and state it.
- Lists of three for rhetorical effect ("courage, wisdom, and clarity") have a high AI-detection rate. Use lists when content actually has three items, not for cadence.

## Quotes

Every quote on every page must be:

1. **Real** — a line the named person actually wrote or said.
2. **Cited** with author, work, chapter/letter/fragment, translator, and year. Example: `Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 7.59 (trans. George Long, 1862)`.
3. **In an archaic translation** for consistency. The site uses public-domain Victorian and Edwardian translations: Long for Marcus, Gummere for Seneca, Legge for Confucius, Giles for the Tao Te Ching, Long for Epictetus.

Source verification options:
- Project Gutenberg (`gutenberg.org`) — searchable full text of every public-domain translation we use.
- Perseus Digital Library (`perseus.tufts.edu`) — Greek and Latin originals with linked translations.
- The Loeb Classical Library for Seneca and Marcus.

If a quote shows up only on a Goodreads quotes page or a "Top 100 Stoic Quotes" listicle and not in a primary source, do not use it.

## Newsletter format (also in plan)

Each newsletter is one focused lesson, ~350–450 words, in this shape:

1. **Subject** in frontmatter — 6–12 words, sentence case, no exclamation marks.
2. **Preview text** in frontmatter — one line, hints at the substance without giving it away.
3. **Headline** — short, often two words. Sits at the top of the email.
4. **Opening paragraph** — source moment, misreading correction, definition, echo, principle stated plainly, or biographical contrast. (Never scene-setting.)
5. **Block quote** with full citation.
6. **Two or three paragraphs of essay** unpacking the line.
7. **Try this today.** — one concrete practice, doable in under 10 minutes.
8. **Sign-off** — `Stoic Meditations` on its own line.
9. **P.S.** — optional, one sentence linking back to the book or signaling the next lesson.
