# The fountain

Marcus ruled Rome from 161 to 180 CE and spent most of those years on the Danube frontier prosecuting a war while the Antonine Plague swept the empire. The Meditations are not a treatise. They are his private working journal, written in Greek, on campaign, never meant for anyone else to read. This line is one of the ones he wrote to himself when he needed it.

> "Look within. Within is the fountain of good, and it will ever bubble up, if thou wilt ever dig."
>
> Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 7.59 (trans. George Long, 1862)

The good he names is not a feeling. It is the working set of inner resources a serious adult uses to get through a day. The ability to think clearly under pressure. To choose your response. To refuse what would diminish you. To act well when no one is watching. These are not handed out by status, wealth, or luck. They come standard with being human. Most people never use them, because they have been trained to look outside themselves for the same goods.

Marcus is the emperor of the known world when he writes this. He has armies, scribes, gold, and the obedience of millions. If anything could be arranged externally, he could arrange it. None of it produced the steadiness he needed. He writes the line to himself, in private, because the answer was not out there for him either, and it is not out there for the rest of us.

The dig is small and daily. A few quiet minutes before reaching for the phone. One honest sentence in a notebook before the day starts pulling at you. One pause before the reactive sentence leaves your mouth. Repeated over weeks, it produces a person who can meet the day from inside rather than chase it from outside.

**Try this today.** Take five quiet minutes before opening your phone. Do not do anything with the time. Just notice what comes up in the space when nothing is being pushed at you. That is the column the digging happens in.
