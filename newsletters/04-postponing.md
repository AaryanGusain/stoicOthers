# Dum differtur vita transcurrit

Seneca opens his entire collection of letters to Lucilius with this line because he knew his correspondent, like the rest of us, would understand the disease before he could be persuaded of the cure. *Letter 1* is not about anything in particular. It is about how Lucilius is using his time. Seneca puts it first because, in his view, every other ethical question comes after this one.

> "While we are postponing, life speeds by."
>
> Seneca, Moral Letters to Lucilius 1.1 (trans. Richard Mott Gummere, 1917)

The Latin word here is *differre*, to defer, to push out, to set the matter aside until a more convenient moment. Seneca is not warning about laziness. Lucilius was a senior Roman official, by no measure idle. The warning is for the energetic person, the diligent person, the person whose calendar is full of useful things but whose actual life is being lived in the gaps between them.

What we postpone is rarely the thing we say is important. We postpone what we are afraid of doing badly. The letter we owe a friend. The honest conversation. The half-built project that has been waiting since spring. They wait while easier work fills the morning. Seneca's point is that the wait is not free. The hours have not been saved. They have been spent.

Two years from now, the thing you postponed in October will still be the thing you postponed in October. Time has done its work on it. You will have done none.

**Try this today.** Pick one thing you have been postponing for more than two months. Do not try to finish it. Just convert it from intention to motion. Send the first message. Open the file. Write the first paragraph. Five minutes is enough. The point is that today is the day it stopped being postponed.
