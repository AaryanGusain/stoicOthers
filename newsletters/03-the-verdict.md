# The verdict

This is the most-quoted single move in Stoic practice, the one that became the explicit source of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy two thousand years later. Aaron Beck, who founded CBT in the 1960s, named Epictetus as a direct influence. Marcus said it cleaner. He said it to himself, on a frontier campaign, in the half-light between dispatches.

> "If thou art pained by any external thing, it is not this thing that disturbs thee, but thy own judgment about it. And it is in thy power to wipe out this judgment now."
>
> Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 8.47 (trans. George Long, 1862)

The line is easy to misread as denial. It is not. Marcus is not saying the things outside you are not real. He buried children. The plague was real. The frontier was real. He is making a narrower, more useful point about where the suffering actually lives.

Between any event and your response to it, there is a verdict. The verdict happens fast, often in the half-second between the message arriving and your stomach dropping, often without your noticing it happen. The verdict is made of words like *they think less of me, this means the project is failing, this confirms what I feared*. The event itself contains none of that language. You wrote it.

Once the verdict is seen, it becomes editable. Not always easy. Editable. Most of what spoils an ordinary day is the second layer, the layer made of opinion, riding on the back of the first.

**Try this today.** Catch one moment of irritation. Write the event in one sentence. Write the verdict you passed on it in another. Read them next to each other. The distance between the two sentences is where the work lives, and where the second sentence can be revised.
