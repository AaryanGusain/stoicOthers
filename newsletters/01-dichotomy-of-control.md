# Two columns

Epictetus was born a slave in Hierapolis and lived part of his life in Rome before the emperor Domitian banished philosophers from the city. He settled in Nicopolis and taught there for the rest of his life, writing nothing. His student Arrian transcribed the lectures and condensed them into a portable handbook called the Enchiridion. The first line of that handbook is below. Almost nothing else in Stoic practice works until you have absorbed it.

> "Of things some are in our power, and others are not. In our power are opinion, movement towards a thing, desire, aversion; in a word, whatever are our own acts. Not in our power are the body, property, reputation, offices, and in a word, whatever are not our own acts."
>
> Epictetus, Enchiridion 1 (trans. George Long, 1877)

What is in your power, in his account, is small and entirely yours: your opinions, what you reach for, what you turn from, what you actually do. What is not in your power is huge and constantly mistaken for yours. Your body in the long run. Your reputation always. The weather. The traffic. The verdict of someone else's mood. The outcome of an election.

Most adult suffering comes from confusing the two columns. The afternoon goes badly because we were arguing with weather. The relationship goes badly because we were trying to install opinions in another person's head. The career goes badly because we were watching reputation more closely than work.

The discipline is not resignation. It is a relentless correction of address. Every hour spent fighting what is not yours can be redirected into the smaller column. The smaller column is where leverage actually lives.

**Try this today.** Take three minutes and write down the two or three things weighing on you right now. For each one, mark which column it belongs to. The point is not to fix anything yet. It is to notice how often the heaviest things on the page live in the column you do not control.
