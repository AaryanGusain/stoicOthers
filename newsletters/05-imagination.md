# The rehearsal

Lucilius had written to Seneca about anxiety, in a register most modern readers would recognize without translation. Seneca's reply is famous, and the line below is its central claim. Notice he does not say bad things never happen. He says that the imagination is a far more expensive instrument than the world itself.

> "There are more things, Lucilius, likely to frighten us than there are to crush us; we suffer more often in imagination than in reality."
>
> Seneca, Moral Letters to Lucilius 13.4 (trans. Richard Mott Gummere, 1917)

This is a structural observation about how minds work, not a pep talk. The world tends to deliver events one at a time, narrower than the catastrophe the mind staged the night before. Five separate worries get rehearsed in bed. One or two might happen, never all at once, rarely in the order predicted. The mind charges full price for losses you will never actually take.

The cost of the rehearsal is itself real suffering. Hours that could have gone to sleep, to work, to the person across the table. The body floods itself with the same chemistry it would have used if the event had happened. The bill is paid in advance, every night, for losses that mostly never come due.

Stoicism is not asking you to suppress fear. It is asking you to notice that fear's accounting department has been overcharging you for years, and that the bill is itself part of what you are afraid of.

**Try this today.** Pick one fear you have been rehearsing this week. Write it down in one sentence. Beside it, write what you would actually do in the first hour if it happened. Most fears get smaller when there is an actual plan attached, because most of the fear was the absence of a plan, dressed as a catastrophe.
